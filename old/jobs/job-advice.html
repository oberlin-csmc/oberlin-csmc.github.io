Preparing For the Job Search

Because the job-search may be a lengthy process, it should be approached with the same understanding and organization as with any important project. Prior to beginning the job-search campaign, there are several basic questions which you should ask yourself:

  1. Am I in the right frame of mind to begin this search? Have you resolved the negative feelings about your termination? Have you regained control of your emotional highs and lows? If not, concentrate on the positive aspects of your job-search, it can be a positive and rewarding experience.
  2. Have I established realistic goals? Do you know what kind of work and what position you want? Do you want to, or can you relocate? Do you know who you want to work for and where? If you have not established any goals, you might use the following as a guideline for developing some preliminary goals:
    • Size of company?
    • Its products and, or services?
    • Is it in the private or public sector?
    • Its corporate image and reputation?
    • Its annual or projected growth?
    • Its geographical location?
  3. Am I in need of professional counseling? If you are suffering from chronic depression and still feel stunned and desperate several months after the loss of your job, you may want to seek expert help to work through your problem. Frequently, just a few sessions with a counselor will help you regain your old confidence and drive to succeed.
  4. Am I prepared to work hard at the job-search? An effective job-search is a full-time job. You will need to spend at least four to six hours a day on the search. You will be your own marketing manager, public relations expert, and administrative assistant. It is up to you to do it all. Be sure to keep good records so as not to duplicate your efforts.
  5. Am I ready to start my search with confidence? Using the results of your personal asset evaluation, review the positive aspects of your career and accomplishments. This exercise will serve to reinforce your sense of self-worth and give focus to what sort of position you are most qualified for.
  6. Do I have the necessary technical and administrative support for my job-search? Do you have an orderly place to work? If you can't type, do you have access to a word processor or a paid secretary or devoted friend who will stick with you and help? Obtain white or cream color rag-content stationary with easily readable typeface for your name and address. Also, you will need to have office supplies, such as: scissors, small postage scale, stamps, manila envelopes, pens and pencils, typewriter of some kind, typing paper, yellow pads, blank mailing labels, Scotch tape, and three-by-five index cards.

Developing A Marketing Strategy

What is a marketing strategy? It is your plan or means of seeking and getting a job by using:

  • Published openings and non-published positions (hidden jobs).
  • Executive search firms and employment agencies.
  • Target company mailings and industry mailings.
  • Personal contact network.
  • Employment advertisements.
Even while you are preparing your marketing tools and becoming proficient in interviewing, you should be gathering information on target companies which you wish to approach during your job campaign.

Published Openings

A published opening is a job vacancy that has been officially declared open within a company. Typically, this is announced by the issuance of a personnel requisition through the personnel department. It is made known publicly through some kind of publication or solicitation in a newspaper, magazine, or by posting within the company. These are called published, even though they sometimes are hard to uncover. At this point, the company is actively taking steps to fill the position. Therefore, if you contact the company and appear to be qualified, you will have a good chance of getting an interview.

Hidden Jobs

Hidden jobs refer to a part of the job market in which the positions have not been advertised or made known to the general public. Some examples include:

  • A department that has fallen behind in its assigned responsibility and requires immediate additional staffing.
  • Jobs which are created because of the development of a new product, service or company growth, and expansion.
  • Confidential employee replacement requirement.

Planning your Job Search Campaign

The following sections will discuss specific courses of action for seeking out both types of jobs, the published ones and the hidden ones.

Employment Agencies

Almost all companies, at one time or another, make use of employment agencies to support their personnel department activities and requirements. When dealing with employment agencies it is important to know:

  • Employment agencies typically deal with jobs from entry level to $50,000 per year.
  • Most of them specialize in certain types of positions or vocations.
  • A few will require you to sign a contract that commits you to pay a fee based upon a certain percent of your income after you accept a job. This fee can be from 10% to 15% of your starting yearly salary. Even if you quit your job you will still be required to pay.
  • It is becoming more common for the employer to pay the agency fee. If using an agency, make sure the employer has agreed to pay the fee.
  • The amount of effort an employment agency spends on trying to get you a job is a function of how marketable they feel you are. If you fit one or more of their client's profiles, they will arrange for interviews right away.
  • Remember, employment agencies make their money by placing as many individuals as possible, in the shortest period of time. Be cautious, they may push you to take something that you don't really want. This is frequently done so they can increase their volume of placements.
  • Be aware that agencies can also work against you by soliciting companies which you have already interviewed with and presenting other candidates they represent. Never let them know what your job-search campaign plan is and don't let them know you are covering the market on your own.

You should also know that when working through an employment agency, the prospective employer will be required to pay a fee of 20% to 30% of your first year's salary. Given the choice between two equally qualified individuals, the company will most likely select the one which has no fee associated with employment. It is important to use agencies as ONE of the strategies of your job-search. They are aware of open positions and can get you interviews. They can also provide you with feedback on your interviewing skills and how a company might react to you.

Search Firms

Search firms work primarily for the employer. They work on either a retainer or contingency basis. Employers might pay them a retainer to locate qualified candidates for a specific position. Other searches are done on a contingency basis where the firm only receives a fee if the company hires an employee they have represented.

Once you submit your resume, the search firm will contact you by telephone if your background matches the needs of an ongoing search assignment. During the initial telephone conversation, the identity of the prospective employer will not be revealed. Nevertheless, sufficient information will be exchanged to give you an idea about the company. (i.e., size, industry, services, products, location, etc.).

Newspaper Advertisements

Large metropolitan newspapers have ads in their business section which lists job openings. In some cases the name of the company is concealed with only a box number given to respond to.

Although answering ads in the newspaper is a somewhat low-yield effort, there may be an occasional opening that is particularly suited to your experience and education. When responding to these ads, the following should be taken into consideration.

  • Identify the key assets the advertiser is seeking and briefly list them on paper.
  • Identify as many of your own assets and skills which reasonably match the stated requirements.
  • Use both lists of needs and assets to address the position in the first part of your response letter.
  • The rest of your response letter should be short and to the point, and refer to an attached resume.
  • Avoid the issue of salary requirements by leaving it out altogether or if required, by giving a broad salary range.
  • A particular ad in which you are interested should be answered whether you are qualified, over qualified, or under qualified for it. Companies rarely find a perfect match to their stated requirements.
  • Answer advertisements from companies which you might have responded to before. Companies generally don't have very accurate tracking and filing systems for applicants. Remember, one letter expressing no interest does not mean no interest forever.
  • Respond to companies who run large advertisements and do a considerable amount of hiring. Even if you don't fit the requirements for the advertised opening, you may fit another unadvertised opening.
  • Don't overlook blind or box number only advertisements. Even though the success rate is minimal, they sometimes do pan out.

Targeted Companies

Target companies are the ones which you really want to work for. They should be chosen according to the goals and requirements that you have developed, including location, working climate, profitability, opportunity for promotion, pay scales, etc. These companies should be approached with a tailor-made job-search campaign. Remember, the bigger the market for your particular skills or pay level, the more selective you can be in defining your target companies.
